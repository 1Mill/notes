### Scaling FAST Agile

* Need >= 8 people to form a product collective, else practicies (e.g. dynamic re-squading) do not make practical sense
* FAST = XP = Open Space (self organization)
* Don't force squads to break up, squad affinity builds psychological safety
* Form collective around products first (e.g. value streams), and then code second (e.g. platform capabilities), but never technology (e.g. frontend, backend, Ruby, Go, etc.)
* Nobody should be expected to know everything and changing squads is an opportunity for growth for those who seek it
* Each squad owns own rituals seperately from the collective

### Value Based Governence

* Orgs have limited capacity
* Product bets are the solution -> Turn ideas into financial models and hypothesized value over a 5 year period

* Piolet programs to validate market interest to hedge risks
* Shift conversations from "ship this thing" to making the right bets
* REFERENCE: Pirate Metrics (AARRR metrics; Acquisition, Activation, Retention, Referral, and Revenue)
  * Easy to validate interest on AA funnel before investing in retention
  * Helpful to attribute gained profts to specific features / product lines because of activiation and retention rates.

* Adapability > Predictibility
* REFERENCE: Art of Agile Development (Jim Shore)
* A team owns a bet, not "done" until value is realized (not just shipped). Only when done-done do they take on a new bet.
* Build trust by doing what you say when you are going to do it, but change what needs to get done (e.g. building an organization which ships mostly profitable features vs ship this specific feature)

### Discovery Trees

* Last responsible momment design
* Emergent design
* What is the next small step we need to take?
* What is the next behavior slice we need the product to do?
* What is the next most important thing for us to do next?
* Do we have enough information to start working on this? If no, break down into smaller chunks.
* Discover the work through doing the work

* Keep top-level delivered value / stories, delete sub-tasks to keep trees clean (some folks disagree)
* Easily visualizes the known work + rough percentage of done (e.g. 3/5 deliverables done -> ~60% done)
* Avoid big design up front
* A leaf should take <= 2 hours to complete

* Antipatterns
  * Huge trees means features too big, need to break into smaller deliverable chunks.
    * Big stories mean big committments, lose ability to pivot (i.e. agility)
  * Keep adding more requirements -> Scope creep
  * Creating the whole tree upfront -> Think only about the next immediate thing you can to do in <= 2 hours

* Makes it easy to communicate status
